The Good News About Your Business

You might be worried that you'll have to sell your business or give up part of it. Here's the key thing to understand:

Key Concept

Your business is almost entirely "personal goodwill" — which means in Colorado, it likely cannot be divided or given a dollar value for the divorce. Here's why courts see it that way:

  • The business is named after you — it's your name and reputation
  • You take all the photos yourself
  • You do all the editing yourself
  • Your clients are hiring you, not a transferable brand
  • If you stopped working tomorrow, there'd be no business to sell

Courts split the value of a business into two categories. Here's what they are and why the distinction matters so much for you:

Type of Value What It Means Can It Be Split?
Enterprise Goodwill Value that exists without the owner — brand systems, employee teams, patents, recurring contracts Yes — marital asset
Personal Goodwill Value tied to you as a person — your reputation, your relationships, your skill No — not divisible in CO
What this means for you: A wedding photography business where the owner is the sole photographer, editor, and the business bears their name is the textbook case for personal goodwill. The actual property that could be divided is just the tangible stuff — camera equipment, maybe a computer — and that's it.
Your Income

What About the $83,000?

Your annual profit matters, but not the way you might think. It doesn't make the business "worth" more for property division. Instead, it factors into spousal maintenance (what some states call alimony).

How Colorado Calculates Maintenance

Colorado uses a formula based on both spouses' incomes:

40% of the higher earner's gross income minus 50% of the lower earner's gross income

How long it lasts depends on how long you were married. Marriages under 3 years may get no maintenance. Longer marriages get more months.

This formula applies whether you're the higher or lower earner — and it turns out, this is important for you. Keep reading.

Important: The $83K represents your labor. You earn it by showing up, shooting, and editing. It's not passive income from an asset — it's your paycheck. A good attorney will make sure the court sees it that way.
The House

What Happens to the House

If you bought the house together during the marriage, it's a marital asset — regardless of whose name is on the deed. Colorado divides marital property "equitably," which means fairly but not necessarily 50/50.

The Three Options

  1. Sell it and split the equity. Most straightforward. After paying off the mortgage and closing costs, you each get a share.
  2. One person buys the other out. This means refinancing the mortgage into one name and paying the other person their share of equity.
  3. Trade assets. One person keeps the house in exchange for giving up their claim on other assets (retirement accounts, savings, etc.).
Colorado Law

How Colorado Handles This

Equitable Distribution State

Colorado is not a 50/50 community property state. It's an "equitable distribution" state. The court divides things fairly, considering factors like:

  • Each person's financial situation after the divorce
  • How much each person contributed to acquiring assets
  • Whether one person was a homemaker (this counts as contribution)
  • Whether either person wasted or hid marital assets
  • Whether there are children and who they'll live with

No Prenup — What That Means

Without a prenup, everything acquired during the marriage is presumed marital property. Things you owned before the marriage are "separate property" — but any increase in their value during the marriage could be considered marital.

Since you started the business during the marriage, the entire business was built within the marriage. But remember — "personal goodwill" still protects you.

Child Support

Child Support — This Is the Big One

With three children and your spouse earning significantly more than you, you will receive child support. This is calculated by Colorado's formula based on:

  • Both parents' gross incomes
  • The number of children (three, in your case)
  • How many overnights the kids spend with each parent
  • Health insurance and childcare costs
Why This Matters So Much

With a $40,000+ income gap and three kids, child support will likely be a meaningful monthly amount. This is probably the single most significant ongoing financial piece of this divorce. The exact number depends on your custody arrangement, which is why that's one of the follow-up questions below.

Even with 50/50 custody, the higher-earning parent still pays child support in Colorado. The amount is just lower than it would be with primary custody.

The PERA Pension

This Could Be One of the Biggest Assets

You mentioned a PERA (Colorado Public Employees' Retirement Association) account. Since you're a self-employed photographer, this is almost certainly your spouse's pension through a government job.

Important

A PERA pension for someone who has worked in Colorado government for much of a 16-year marriage could be extremely valuable — potentially worth more than the house equity. It has two parts:

  • A defined benefit pension — guaranteed monthly income in retirement
  • A 401(k) component — an investment account with a cash balance

The portion that was earned during your 16-year marriage is marital property and can be divided. This gives you significant bargaining power — for example, you might trade your share of the PERA for keeping the house.

Critical deadline: Dividing a PERA requires a specific Domestic Relations Order (DRO) — not a regular QDRO. It must be filed within 90 days of the divorce decree being entered. Missing this deadline can mean losing your right to part of the pension.

Make sure your attorney knows about the PERA and the 90-day window. This is not something to handle last.
